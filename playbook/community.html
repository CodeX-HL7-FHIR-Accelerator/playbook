Building Community

Gathering the right organizations and people has been the most foundational and challenging track in the CodeX Playbook.

It’s very likely that almost everyone reviewing this site agrees that enabling true health data interoperability is more a social challenge, than a medical or technical challenge. It takes people who share a vision and mission, can align different views of how to get there, and invest the resources needed to change the status quo such that the mission gains sufficient momentum to substantially improve health for everyone.

Actions that CodeX has found indispensable include (a) Champions, (b) Governance, and (c) Community Growth. These actions are expanded upon below.

Champions

An essential first step is to gather specialty Champions that share a vision, are committed to lead, and have broad constituencies and access to resources. The specialty as a whole and each Use Case (projects) within the specialty initiative need Champions.

In CodeX, Champions are organizations and individuals within those organization who:

  • Commit to lead
  • Understand the challenges for which data interoperability would help
  • Believe the overall vision approach is a key part of future solutions, e.g. collect patient data once, and directly use as consented for many purposes (graphic at right).
  • Command the attention of their larger community and can influence the future users and stakeholders to engage
  • Motivate vendor implementation of new standards for tested Use Cases
  • Motivate the change needed to use and benefit from of the standards-based systems in use in health processes
  • Muster the resources needed to succeed
  • Drive one more key tracks of the initiative: Community Engagement, Use Case Prioritization, Standards Development, Implementation & Pilots, Adoption & Value

It's clear that Champions have significant responsibilities. But they also appreciate the future benefits in terms of better health care and research.

Large, medical professional societies and Government organizations have been the most frequent CodeX Champions for most Use Cases, but other stakeholders have also been effective Champions. The founding CodeX Champions were American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO), with reach to all oncologists and centers; and The MITRE Corporation, with deep expertise in convening, FHIR standards and systems engineering.

Governance

An organizational framework is important for any initiative involving many organizations, often with divergent interests and views. Views and interests need to heard and understood. Decisions need to be taken and services provided to facilitate work. Examples include updates to the initial Governance structure, Membership structure and fees, adjudicating conflicts, administrative support, starting new Use Case work, and providing funding and resources to projects in special cases when the Community can’t.

CodeX Accelerator provides the Governance for Use Cases around cancer (and the mCODE standard), cardiovascular disease, and genomics, so far. Other governance schemes are possible but would require more effort to start and to coordinate effectively across specialties.

The graphic below captures the CodeX Governance structure as of 2024. The tiered structure offers participation different of levels of responsibility and time commitment. Details of the current CodeX Governance scheme can be found on their website.

A few points to note: Major decisions are made by the elected Steering Committee. The vast majority of all effort is within the Use Case projects. The Community of Practice (near bottom) is open to the public and has been an effective feeder of new organizations and talents into the CodeX membership and Use Cases. One of the key “External Expert Groups” (circle on right) is the mCODE Executive Committee and its Technical Review Group. This Committee provide most of the subject matter expertise across oncology fields that ensure the mCODE FHIR Implementation Guide is based on the most credible and recent clinical knowledge.

A strategy for sustaining work of the initiative is important. Success is not achieved overnight. In CodeX, the range of skills (and sometimes money) needed to drive each Use Case, is provided by participants. Funding for Program Management (near top of the graphic above) is largely from Membership fees. Community Growth

For Use Case projects to progress from a vision to adoption and value in the real world, a variety of organizations and skillsets are needed. Thus, the community must be grown such that there are participants with direct experience related to each of the envisioned roles Use Cases.

For example, in the CodeX Radiation Therapy Case Study, the team included radiation therapy societies, health systems, radiation oncologists, radiation physicists, payers, and vendor. Skillsets included clinical practice, physics, software engineering, informaticists, FHIR developers, HL7 standards process experts, etc. Getting the right set of people together at the right times in the process can be challenging. It’s ideal to have redundancy among these roles, in case some people have to reduce their effort.

The good news is that a promising Use Case has the potential to attract new participants. These participants appreciate the potential value of the work, are willing to invest resources to realize that value, and want to be at the table where decisions are made and work is executed.

CodeX experience suggests that different value propositions often resonate with different stakeholders. The following generalizes the values to a specialty data standardization initiative for involvement six different types of stakeholders, and the value propositions that often resonate with the same stakeholders.

Providers, Health Systems & Associated Specialty Societies

Value to the specialty standardization initiative of engagement by these stakeholders

  • Representing, informing, & convincing a broad group of clinical constituents that this is important
  • Providing experts who can offer deep clinical knowledge
  • Customers of vendors, who they can demand to implement the standards
  • Potential funding

Value to these stakeholders to engage in the specialty initiative

  • As the implementers of care quality improvements through SHR, provider partnerships will bring discoveries made in mCODE to informed patient care.
  • Providers understand the gaps in care and how best to leverage lessons learned from the point of care to inform the SHR development and accelerating improvements based on standardize data.
  • SHR aims to empower shared decision-making between patients and providers.

Patient & Associated Advocate Societies

Value to the specialty standardization initiative of engagement by these stakeholder

  • Represent, inform, convince a broad group of patients and caregivers
  • Provide the patient perspective to ensure patient-centered approach
  • Patients likely to increasingly demand the ability to collect, share and control their data (and thus motivate standards adoption)

Value to these stakeholders to engage in the specialty initiative

  • Patients and caregivers feel the pain of the lack of standardized data (difficult to access, share between providers and caregivers, etc.), and the consequences of this lack. Patients and advocates are just starting to organize to act to address these issues.
  • SHR motto is that “every patient’s journey improves all future care”.
  • Patients advocates can ensure patient centered care is at the forefront of developing core SHR data models.
  • Involvement of patients and advocates will translate to better care in the future, ensuring the continuum of care is complete.

Regulators

Value to the specialty standardization initiative of engagement by these stakeholders

  • Some government agencies issue suggestions and rules/regulations regarding adoption of standards
  • Many government agencies execute in the other grouping on this page
  • Potential funding

Value to these stakeholders to engage in the specialty initiative

  • SHR is aligned with Government rules, regulations, norms (e.g., USCDI, FHIR)
  • Government agencies (ONC, etc.) have already recognized the value of mCODE and the SHR approach
  • The SHR approach will engage with regulators to ensure interests are aligned and implementations are effective.
  • Regulations along the SHR implementation path will be addressed during the summit, along with input from key regulatory bodies themselves.

Vendors (EHR and other health IT companies)

Value to the specialty standardization initiative of engagement by these stakeholders

  • Will respond when many key customers ask for products based on standards
  • Have ability to look across customers and specialties and suggest ways to develop standards to optimize implementation and maintenance in systems

Value to these stakeholders to engage in the specialty initiative

  • Vendors already enabling USCDI sharing via FHIR, and some are implementing mCODE.
  • Customers will increasingly demand SHR.
  • Vendors can help align customer interests (fewer custom implementations).
  • Vendors can provide input to ensure standards are implementable in their systems and consistent across specialties (cost effective development)
  • Vendors who participate actively have a first mover advantage, with SHR-based products early to market.

Payers

Value to the specialty standardization initiative of engagement by these stakeholders

  • Can demand use of standards by associated health systems and vendors
  • Potential funding

Value to these stakeholders to engage in the specialty initiative

  • Easier access to SHR clinical data, standardized across customers needed to make decisions.
  • Improved automation to process actions more quickly, and even automatically (e.g., prior authorization, claims processing, etc.)
  • Higher quality care, supported by standard data, will be informed by payers who are at the forefront of pay-for-value approaches to cancer.

Researchers

Value to the specialty standardization initiative of engagement by these stakeholders

  • Substantial increase in speed, ease and cost-effectiveness when much of the data is collected and shared via the same standards
  • Potential funding

Value to these stakeholders to engage in the specialty initiative

  • will be made readily available for research, with gold standard clinical trial quality.
  • ... Through SHR, patient point-of-care data will be unlocked (with appropriate consents) will be able to be aggregated faster and with less expense, and more quickly available for research.
  • More data
  • Substantial increase in speed, ease and cost-effectiveness when much of the data is collected and shared via the same standards
  • Potential funding
